# Harborline — exception triage that survives a container ship

**Role**: sole product designer · **Team**: 4 engineers, 1 PM · **Year**: 2026

Harborline's fulfilment teams worked exceptions in a 14-column table sorted
by creation time. During the March carrier switch, a single delayed vessel
created 340 exceptions in one morning — and the team triaged them oldest
first, which meant the perishables at the bottom spoiled while someone
re-labeled stationery at the top.

## What I learned before designing

Two days shadowing the Rotterdam team, five interviews across shifts:

- Nobody read the table left to right. Everyone scanned for **customer names
  they feared** — the accounts where one late delivery risks the contract.
- The real triage question wasn't "what's oldest?" but **"what gets worse if
  I ignore it for an hour?"**
- The team kept a paper list of "carrier promises" the system had no field
  for. The paper list was the actual source of truth.

## The bet

Severity-first, not time-first: a queue that leads with **what decays**
(perishables, promised callbacks, contract accounts) and demotes everything
that merely waits. Time in queue became a secondary signal, shown as decay
bars instead of timestamps.

## What shipped

- A three-band queue — *acts now / today / can wait* — with band membership
  explained on every card, because trust in an opaque ranking lasts one
  wrong call.
- Carrier promises became a first-class field with a countdown, replacing
  the paper list.
- Bulk actions only within a band, never across bands — cross-band bulk was
  how the old table caused the stationery-before-perishables incident.

## Results

After eight weeks: median time-to-first-action fell **11 → 4 minutes**;
missed carrier promises fell by roughly a third; the paper list retired
itself. The ranking-explanation cards tested as the single most-trusted
element — several operators read them aloud when justifying calls to
supervisors.

## What I'd do differently

The decay bars read as progress bars for two of five test participants —
"filling up" reads as *good* in most software. A labeled countdown chip
would have been less elegant and more honest.
